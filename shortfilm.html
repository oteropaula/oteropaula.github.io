Context

What is the Polireto?

About POLIWOOD & the Polireto

POLIWOOD is UPV's audiovisual post-production group, a space to develop as an artist across specialisations including video, image and audio editing, digital music composition, VFX and 3D design, with the aim of building a platform that bridges entertainment and knowledge. The Polireto is its internal short film competition: teams have a set window of weeks to write, shoot, and edit a complete short film, culminating in the Poligala - a public screening ceremony held in the ETSIT auditorium where teams present their films and take the stage to discuss their work.

This was my first time participating in the Polireto. I joined POLIWOOD during my third year at UPV and this project pushed me to develop skills I hadn't had the chance to apply seriously before: structuring a story under a tight brief, making editing decisions that serve a narrative rather than just assembling footage, and coordinating with teammates across different technical disciplines.

At the Poligala, my teammate Jordi and I went on stage to present the film and speak about our process - a moment that was as much about communication and public presentation as it was about the filmmaking itself.

Poligala

The screening night

Poligala ceremony - ETSIT auditorium, UPV

On stage at the ETSIT auditorium presenting the short film alongside Jordi. The Poligala is the annual ceremony where all Polireto entries are screened and teams take the stage to discuss their creative process.

The Poligala was a highlight of my year at POLIWOOD. Seeing the finished film on a proper screen in the auditorium - having started from a blank page and a team of three - was genuinely satisfying. The stage presentation pushed me to articulate creative decisions clearly, which turned out to be a different kind of challenge to writing or editing.

Reflection

What I took from it

The Polireto was my first serious experience with the full scriptwriting and editing pipeline - from concept to screened film. The most interesting tension was between what the script imagined and what the edit could actually deliver given the footage. Learning to make those decisions quickly, and to trust the team on the parts that weren't mine (sound, VFX), was the real takeaway.

It also confirmed something I already suspected: the skills that matter most in collaborative creative work aren't purely technical. Keeping the scope realistic, communicating clearly about what you need from teammates, and committing to a version rather than endlessly reworking it - those are the things that get a project finished.
